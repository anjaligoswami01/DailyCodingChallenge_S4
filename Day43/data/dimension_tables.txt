Dimension tables provide descriptive information that gives context to business events stored in fact tables. They are fundamental components of dimensional data modeling.

Common dimension examples include customers, products, employees, locations, suppliers, and dates. These dimensions contain attributes that help users analyze and interpret business data.

A customer dimension might include customer name, age group, city, region, and membership status. Analysts can use these attributes to segment and filter reports.

Dimension tables typically have surrogate keys that uniquely identify records. These keys simplify relationships with fact tables and support historical tracking.

Slowly Changing Dimensions (SCDs) are commonly used to manage changes in dimensional attributes over time. Different SCD types provide varying approaches to preserving historical information.

Dimensions support drill-down analysis. For example, analysts can move from country-level sales to region-level and city-level details using location dimensions.

Data engineers design dimensions carefully to ensure consistency across reports and dashboards. Well-structured dimensions improve usability and analytical flexibility.

Dimension tables are essential for business intelligence systems because they transform raw transactional data into meaningful business insights.
