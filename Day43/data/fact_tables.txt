Fact tables store measurable business events and quantitative metrics within a dimensional data warehouse. They serve as the central component of analytical data models.

Examples of fact table metrics include sales revenue, order quantities, inventory levels, website visits, and customer transactions. Each record typically represents a specific business event.

Fact tables contain foreign keys linking to dimension tables. These relationships provide context for analysis. For example, a sales fact table may reference customer, product, location, and date dimensions.

Facts are generally classified into three categories: additive, semi-additive, and non-additive. Additive facts can be summed across all dimensions, while semi-additive and non-additive facts require special handling.

Granularity refers to the level of detail stored in a fact table. Transaction-level granularity provides maximum flexibility but may increase storage requirements.

Data engineers carefully define granularity because it affects reporting capabilities, performance, and storage costs.

Partitioning, indexing, and efficient storage formats help optimize large fact tables for analytical workloads.

Fact tables enable organizations to measure performance, monitor operations, and generate business insights. They form the foundation of dashboards, reports, and advanced analytics systems.

Understanding fact table design is essential for building scalable and high-performing data warehouse architectures.
